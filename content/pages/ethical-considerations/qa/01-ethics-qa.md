## What's the most important ethical principle to apply when adopting AI tools in schools?

I'd say **do no harm first, then do good**. Before asking "how can this tool benefit our students?", ask "what could go wrong, and for whom?" Schools that start with this question are more likely to catch privacy issues, bias risks, and equity concerns before they cause harm.

Concretely: require a data privacy review for every AI tool before adoption. Ask vendors hard questions. Default to *not* adopting a tool if you can't get satisfactory answers.

## How should schools handle students using AI without permission?

With care, proportionality, and a learning orientation rather than a punitive one. The first question should be: did the student understand the policy? AI use norms are evolving rapidly, and many students are genuinely uncertain about where the line is.

If there's a clear policy violation, the response should focus on understanding *why* the student used AI — was it lack of confidence? Time pressure? A perception that the assignment was busywork? — and addressing those root causes, not just the behavior.

Harsh academic penalties for AI use, without thoughtful policy design and clear communication, are likely to criminalize normal student behavior and disproportionately harm students who lack strong advocate adults in their lives.

## Is it ethical to use AI to grade student work?

I think AI-assisted grading can be ethical under certain conditions: the rubric is clear and public, the AI is used for low-stakes or formative work rather than high-stakes summative assessment, and a human reviews any grade before it is communicated to the student.

What I find ethically problematic is using AI to make consequential decisions about students — final grades, promotion, placement — without meaningful human review and clear appeals processes.
