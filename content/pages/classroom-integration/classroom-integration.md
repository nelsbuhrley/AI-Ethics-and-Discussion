# Classroom Integration

Integrating AI into the classroom is not a single event — it's an ongoing process of experimentation, reflection, and adjustment. This page explores what that process looks like in practice.

## Where AI Is Already Present

Many schools are already using AI without necessarily labeling it as such. Spell-checkers, grammar tools, reading-level classifiers, and adaptive math platforms all incorporate AI. The difference today is that **generative AI** — capable of producing original text, images, code, and more — has crossed a capability threshold that makes it both far more powerful and far more disruptive.

## Practical Use Cases for Teachers

### Lesson Planning
AI can generate lesson plan drafts in seconds. A teacher can describe their learning objective, their students' approximate reading level, and any constraints (time, materials, standards alignment), and receive a structured plan to react to rather than build from scratch. This shifts the teacher's role from creator of scaffolding to *editor and judge of scaffolding*.

### Differentiated Instruction
One of the hardest challenges in teaching is meeting students where they are. AI can generate multiple versions of the same reading passage at different Lexile levels, or reframe an explanation in simpler language instantly. This doesn't eliminate the need for teacher judgment, but it reduces a time barrier.

### Formative Feedback
AI tools can provide students with instant, non-judgmental feedback on drafts — flagging unclear sentences, suggesting stronger evidence, or asking Socratic questions. This frees the teacher to focus face-to-face feedback time on higher-order thinking.

### Administrative Efficiency
From writing sub plans to summarizing parent communications, AI reduces the invisible overhead of teaching that often extends the workday well into evening hours.

## What Doesn't Work (Yet)

AI struggles with **nuance, context, and relationship**. It cannot read the room. It cannot sense that a student's flat affect today is because something difficult happened at home. It cannot make the real-time decision to abandon today's plan because the class stumbled onto a genuinely rich tangent.

It also struggles with accuracy in specialized domains. AI-generated content in science, history, and mathematics requires careful fact-checking — a skill that itself needs to be taught to students using these tools.

## A Framework for Thoughtful Integration

I suggest thinking about AI integration along two axes:

| | **Low Stakes** | **High Stakes** |
|---|---|---|
| **AI as Tool** | Draft feedback, generate ideas | Needs human review |
| **AI as Subject** | Discuss what AI can/can't do | Ethical case studies |

Start with low-stakes uses where mistakes are recoverable. As confidence grows, move toward higher-stakes applications — always with human judgment as the final check.
