## How do I introduce AI tools to students who have never used them?

Keep rollout low-risk with a three-step posture: **explore, experiment, evaluate**.

1. Explore: Learn the tool yourself before assigning it to students.
2. Experiment: Pilot it in one lesson or one assignment.
3. Evaluate: Measure learning quality, student engagement, and teacher workload.

Then start with a low-stakes, structured activity where the goal is evaluation, not performance.

For example, ask students to use AI to explain a concept, then compare that explanation against class materials and identify errors, omissions, or oversimplifications. This builds tool familiarity and critical judgment at the same time.

## How can AI help teachers without replacing them?

AI is strongest at repetitive and time-heavy tasks:

- Drafting lesson plans and rubrics
- Creating differentiated examples
- Producing quick formative prompts
- Summarizing long materials for planning

Use AI to generate first drafts, not final plans. Give clear constraints (objective, standards, time, class context), then review for accuracy, appropriateness, and cognitive demand.

Where AI is weakest is exactly where teachers are strongest: relationship, judgment, classroom culture, and motivational coaching.

The best use is to offload low-value prep so teachers can invest more energy in high-value human interaction.

## What's the best way to redesign assignments to account for AI?

Shift toward process-visible assessment.

Instead of grading only the final product, require planning notes, source reasoning, revision reflections, or brief oral defenses. This does not eliminate AI use; it makes student thinking visible.

In-class checkpoints are especially effective because they provide direct evidence of understanding.

## What should administrators do before requiring a new AI platform?

Before adoption, administrators should verify five things:

1. Reliability: Are outputs and scores consistent enough to inform instruction?
2. Training: Do teachers have practical training, not just a product demo?
3. Support: Is there a clear plan for troubleshooting and feedback loops?
4. Policy: Are expectations clear about allowed use, disclosure, process evidence, misuse handling, and accommodations/equitable access?
5. Evaluation and scope: Is there a pilot plan with success metrics (learning, equity, workload), and is the tool framed as support rather than a replacement for teacher judgment?

If any of these are missing, rollout should be limited to pilot status.

## How do I know when an AI tool should be removed from class use?

Remove or pause a tool when it consistently:

- Produces unreliable results in high-impact contexts
- Increases teacher review time instead of decreasing it
- Makes scores or feedback less trustworthy or less transparent
- Interrupts instruction with repeated technical failures
- Leads to more output but less understanding (students can’t explain their work)
- Reduces meaningful student engagement because the activity feels automated

If two or more of these appear consistently, pause and redesign before scaling.

Adoption should be reversible. If a tool does not serve learning, it should not stay in the workflow.
