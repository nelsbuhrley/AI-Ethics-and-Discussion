## How do I introduce AI tools to students who have never used them?

Start with a structured, low-stakes exploration. Give students a short, specific task — summarizing a paragraph, generating five ideas for a project, or explaining a concept in simpler terms — and then have them evaluate the output together as a class. What did the AI get right? What did it get wrong? What surprised you?

This approach builds familiarity while immediately developing critical evaluation skills. It also surfaces students who have already been using AI independently, which is a useful signal for where the class is.

## What's the best way to redesign assignments to account for AI?

The most durable approach is to shift toward **process-visible assessments**. Instead of (or in addition to) a polished final essay, require students to submit a research journal, an annotated draft with reflections on their revision choices, or a short recorded explanation of their argument. These artifacts of process are hard to fake with AI.

In-class writing and discussion-based assessment are also increasingly valuable — not to "catch" AI use, but because they provide direct evidence of student thinking that a take-home essay cannot.

## Can AI help students with learning differences?

Absolutely — and this is one of the most exciting applications. Students with dyslexia can use AI text-to-speech and writing assistance. Students who struggle with executive function can use AI to help break large tasks into smaller steps. English language learners can use AI to translate, clarify vocabulary, and practice writing in a low-stakes environment.

The key is ensuring these tools are available equitably and that students are taught to use them effectively, rather than assuming the technology alone will solve the underlying challenge.
