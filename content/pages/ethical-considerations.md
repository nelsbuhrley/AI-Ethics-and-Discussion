# Ethical Considerations

Every powerful tool raises ethical questions, and AI in education is no exception. Here I explore four areas I consider most important: equity, privacy, bias, and academic integrity.

## Equity

The introduction of any new technology into education risks a familiar pattern: well-resourced schools and students gain access first, use it most effectively, and pull further ahead while under-resourced schools wait.

AI is no different. High-quality AI tutoring tools, personalized learning platforms, and AI-assisted feedback systems cost money. Schools in low-income areas often lack the infrastructure, the professional development funding, and the device ecosystem to deploy these tools effectively.

This is not an argument against AI in education — it's an argument for **intentional policy intervention**. If we allow market forces alone to determine AI access, we will deepen existing inequities. Policymakers, district leaders, and philanthropies need to treat AI access as they would access to textbooks: as a basic educational resource question.

## Privacy

Students are among the most vulnerable populations when it comes to data privacy. They are minors. They cannot fully understand or consent to how their data is used. And their school records — which AI systems inevitably interact with — are protected by law (FERPA in the United States) for good reason.

Recent work on AI ethics |src|'kaplan_ethics'|'short'| emphasizes that ethical frameworks must prioritize data protection in educational contexts.

Key questions every school should ask before adopting an AI tool:

1. **What data is collected?** Does the tool log keystrokes, time on task, or written content?
2. **Where is data stored?** Is it in a compliant cloud infrastructure?
3. **Who has access?** Can the vendor use student data to train its models?
4. **How long is data retained?** Is there a clear deletion policy?
5. **What happens if there's a breach?** Is there a notification plan?

I believe schools should adopt a **data minimization principle**: only deploy tools that collect the minimum data necessary for the educational function.

## Bias

AI systems are trained on data, and data reflects the world as it is — including its biases. This means AI tools can encode and amplify biases around race, gender, socioeconomic status, language, and ability.

A writing feedback tool trained primarily on formal academic English may systematically penalize students who write in African American Vernacular English. A college admissions prediction tool may reflect historical biases in admissions data. An AI tutoring system may make assumptions about learning pace based on demographic patterns.

Addressing bias requires:

- **Diverse training data** — more representative of all students
- **Ongoing auditing** — regular testing for disparate impact across demographic groups
- **Human oversight** — final decisions about students should involve human judgment
- **Transparency** — schools and families should know what signals an AI is using

## Academic Integrity

The rise of AI writing tools has forced a necessary reckoning with what academic integrity actually means. If a student submits an essay written entirely by an AI, have they cheated?

I think the answer depends on the assignment. If the assignment is designed to assess a student's ability to construct an argument, organize evidence, and find their voice — yes, full AI substitution undermines the purpose. If the assignment is designed to assess whether a student understands a concept, an AI-written essay may tell us nothing about that understanding.

The deeper challenge is that **AI detection tools are unreliable**. They produce both false positives (flagging human-written work as AI-generated) and false negatives (missing AI-generated text). Punishing students based on these tools creates real risk of harm, especially for students who write in non-standard dialects.

The more productive response is to redesign assessments so that process, reflection, and in-person performance are central to what we evaluate.
