## How should students talk with instructors about AI use?

Students listen to your teachers, some will happily accept the use of AI in your assignments, others will be strongly opposed to it. Your integrity depends on your transparency with your educators. Be honest in all that you do. I have found that a open discussion about AI with a teacher almost always yields me a understanding of their goals and desires for my time in their class and helps me understand their AI policies better

## What does “using AI ethically” look like for a student?

Use AI to *support* learning, not replace it.

Good uses typically include:

- Explaining a concept in a different way
- Generating practice questions or flashcards
- Checking clarity, structure, or grammar after you’ve drafted your work
- Helping you plan (outlines, study schedules, next steps)

Risky uses are the ones that remove the learning work (thinking, deciding, writing, solving) and leave you with a finished product you can’t reproduce.

## How can I avoid outsourcing my thinking when AI is available?

A practical rule: **attempt first, then consult AI**.

- Try the problem or draft the paragraph on your own.
- Use AI to identify gaps or give feedback.
- Revise with your own judgment and voice.
- Make sure you can explain the final result without the tool.

If you can’t explain it, you didn’t learn it.

## What should I do if a task feels like “busywork” in the AI era?

Don’t treat that as permission to let AI do it for you—treat it as a signal to clarify the purpose.

- Ask: “What skill is this assignment trying to build?”
- If the purpose is unclear, ask your instructor.
- If you have a better alternative that targets the same skill, propose it respectfully.

The goal isn’t to finish faster—it’s to get better.

## Why does human writing still matter if AI can generate text?

Because writing is not only a product—it’s a process.

When you write, you practice organizing ideas, making judgments, and communicating meaning. Even if AI can produce fluent text, it can’t replace the personal growth that comes from wrestling with the material yourself.

## Should I disclose or cite AI use in my assignments?

Default to transparency.

- Follow your teacher’s policy first.
- If the policy is unclear, ask.
- When in doubt, add a short note describing how you used AI (e.g., “Used an AI tool to brainstorm an outline and to proofread a draft; all final wording and arguments are my own.”).

Honesty is usually cheaper than damage control.

## How do I check whether AI output is accurate?

Treat AI like a helpful but unreliable study partner.

- Verify key claims with trusted sources.
- Ask for citations, then *check the citations yourself*.
- Watch for confident language without evidence.
- Compare against what your course materials say.

Accuracy is part of integrity.
