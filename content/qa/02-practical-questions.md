## How should teachers approach AI in their classrooms?

I recommend a three-step posture: **explore, experiment, evaluate**.

First, take time to genuinely understand what the tools can and cannot do. Use them yourself. Try to break them. Second, run small experiments — a single lesson, a single assignment — where AI is part of the process. Reflect honestly on what changed. Third, evaluate: Did student learning improve? Did students engage more deeply, or less? Was your time freed up or consumed by new complexity?

Most importantly, don't feel pressure to have it all figured out. Nobody does. The educators who will serve students best are those willing to stay curious and adapt.

## How can AI help teachers without replacing them?

AI excels at tasks that are time-consuming and formulaic: generating first drafts of rubrics, suggesting differentiated reading passages, summarizing research, providing instant feedback on grammar. These are tasks where an hour of teacher time currently yields limited pedagogical value.

Where AI cannot replace teachers is in the relational and interpretive work: noticing when a student is disengaged for reasons that have nothing to do with the lesson, making judgment calls about when to push and when to support, building the trust that makes a student feel safe to take intellectual risks.

The best outcome is a teacher who has reclaimed time from administrative work and can invest it in the deeply human parts of education.
