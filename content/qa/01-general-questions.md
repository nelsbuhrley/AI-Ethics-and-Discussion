## What do you mean by AI in education?

When I talk about AI in education, I mean tools that can assist with learning, teaching, and school operations. That includes writing assistants, tutoring systems, adaptive practice platforms, analytics dashboards, and workflow tools for educators.

The key distinction is not whether a tool is "AI" in the abstract, but what role it plays in learning. Some tools help students think more deeply. Others make it easier to bypass thinking. That difference matters more than the label.

## Is AI a threat to education?

Not inherently. AI is a force multiplier, and force multipliers can help or harm depending on design and use.

The biggest risks are:

- Students outsourcing deep thinking to AI, missing the productive struggle that builds genuine understanding.
- Schools deploying tools faster than they build policy, training, and accountability.
- Over-trusting low-quality outputs in high-stakes decisions.
- Widening inequity if only well-funded schools can access high-quality tools and support.

The goal should be to treat AI as an instructional support, not an instructional authority.

## How can AI be used to find new ideas?

AI should not be viewed as a machine that "thinks" of ideas for us, but rather as a highly efficient "sandbox" for discovery. By using AI, we can build these sandboxes at an unprecedented scale. AI allows us to rapidly iterate through thousands of permutations and "what-if" scenarios that would be impossible to explore manually. The goal isn't to let the AI provide the final answer, but to use it to generate the unexpected collisions and "wonderful" surprises that a human can then recognize, refine, and claim as a new idea.
